## **Executive Summary (Refined with GPT-5.4 Cyber Context)**

The recent release of advanced cyber-capable AI models, including Anthropic’s Claude Mythos (7 April 2026) and OpenAI’s GPT-5.4 Cyber (announced mid-April 2026), marks a structural inflection point in the cyber threat landscape rather than an isolated technological milestone.

In controlled environments, these models demonstrate the ability to autonomously discover, validate, and exploit software vulnerabilities, including zero-days, and to generate complete exploit chains within minutes. This compresses the traditional exploitation lifecycle from weeks or months to near real-time, fundamentally altering the economics and accessibility of sophisticated cyber-attacks.

Importantly, this capability is no longer confined to a single provider. The parallel emergence of comparable capabilities across leading AI labs signals the establishment of a new baseline in offensive cyber potential, increasing the likelihood of rapid capability diffusion over time.

At present, access to these systems remains partially constrained. For example, OpenAI’s GPT-5.4 Cyber is not publicly available and is being deployed under a restricted **Trusted Access for Cyber (TAC)** programme, limiting usage to vetted organisations. While such controls may temporarily slow proliferation, they are unlikely to prevent eventual broader access, whether through controlled releases, model replication, or downstream tooling.

In parallel, defensive initiatives are emerging. Anthropic’s Project Glasswing (9 April 2026), alongside anticipated equivalents across the ecosystem, demonstrates the dual-use nature of these technologies: the same capabilities enabling rapid exploitation can also be leveraged to identify and remediate vulnerabilities pre-emptively.

For a global systemically important bank, this evolution redefines the speed, scale, and asymmetry of cyber risk. Existing controls such as periodic vulnerability scanning, scheduled patching, and manual threat analysis remain necessary but are no longer sufficient in isolation. The operating model must shift towards continuous, intelligence-driven, and increasingly automated defence.

The strategic implication is clear: cyber resilience must now be designed for an environment where both attackers and defenders operate with near real-time, AI-augmented capabilities.
