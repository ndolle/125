## **1. What Has Changed**

The introduction of AI-driven exploitation fundamentally alters how vulnerabilities are discovered, weaponised, and executed.

Vulnerability discovery, which traditionally relied on skilled human effort over weeks or months, is now largely automated. AI systems can identify weaknesses within minutes and, in some cases, uncover previously unknown vulnerabilities during controlled testing. More importantly, they are capable of linking multiple low-severity issues into a single, high-impact exploit path, significantly increasing the effective attack surface.

At the same time, the speed of attack execution has compressed dramatically. What previously required days or weeks of reconnaissance, development, and validation can now be achieved in minutes. There is evidence that functional exploit chains can be generated in a single execution cycle, effectively removing the natural delays that once gave defenders time to react.

This shift is also lowering the barrier to entry for threat actors. Capabilities that were once limited to highly skilled individuals or well-resourced groups are becoming more widely accessible. As a result, the volume and diversity of potential attackers is increasing, amplifying the likelihood of exploitation attempts across a broader range of targets.

Finally, the defensive model itself is becoming outdated. Traditional approaches based on periodic scanning and scheduled patching are no longer sufficient in an environment where vulnerabilities can be discovered and exploited almost instantly. Effective defence now requires continuous monitoring, AI-assisted detection, and near real-time remediation.

## **3. Risk Assessment and Adverse Scenarios**

AI-driven exploitation materially increases both the **likelihood and impact** of cyber incidents across the bank’s critical operations.

The most immediate risk is operational disruption. The ability to generate rapid exploit chains targeting interconnected systems means that core platforms such as payment processing, AML controls, and digital banking channels could be compromised with little warning. The compression of attack timelines leaves limited opportunity for traditional containment measures, increasing the probability of service outages or degraded operations.

In parallel, the risk of data breach is significantly elevated. AI-assisted exploitation increases the likelihood of successfully identifying and leveraging previously unknown vulnerabilities, including zero-day cases. This creates a credible pathway for unauthorised access to sensitive customer and transactional data, with direct implications for confidentiality, integrity, and trust.

A defining feature of this new threat landscape is the acceleration of attack velocity. Exploitation cycles that previously unfolded over weeks can now occur within minutes. This represents a structural shift in risk, as existing detection and response capabilities are not designed to operate at this speed. As a result, incidents may progress to advanced stages before intervention is possible.

There is also a heightened regulatory exposure. A successful exploit leading to operational disruption or data compromise would likely trigger obligations under frameworks such as GDPR, NIS2, and EBA ICT guidelines. Given the speed and scale of potential incidents, the bank faces increased risk of non-compliance, financial penalties, and reputational damage.

Controlled testing further reinforces the severity of this risk. AI-assisted exploitation has demonstrated success rates of approximately 72% in zero-day scenarios, indicating a step-change from traditional attack effectiveness. Combined with the ability to rapidly construct exploit chains, this suggests that threat actors can achieve meaningful impact with greater consistency and reduced effort.

Taken together, these factors indicate a **high to critical risk environment**, characterised not only by increased threat capability, but by a fundamental mismatch between attack speed and current defensive readiness.
